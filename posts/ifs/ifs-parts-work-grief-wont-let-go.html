IFS Parts Work for Grief: The Part That Won't Let You Let Go

When you feel stuck in grief, Internal Family Systems therapy offers a different explanation than pathology: a part of you is holding on, and it has a reason. This article walks through how to find that part, understand what it is protecting, and help it release without forcing closure.

What is IFS parts work for grief?

Internal Family Systems (IFS), developed by Richard Schwartz, is a therapy model built on the idea that the psyche is made up of multiple parts, each with its own perspective, role, and history. In IFS, grief is not a malfunction. It is carried by one or more parts of the system. When grief feels immovable, IFS says the system is doing its job, protecting you from an experience it does not yet feel safe enough to fully process.

How does IFS explain grief that won't move?

In IFS, two types of parts are usually involved in stuck grief. An exile is the part holding the raw loss, the hurt, the longing, the moment of the loss itself. A protector (often called a manager) shields you from the exile's pain through numbing, staying busy, intellectualizing, or staying in forward momentum. These two parts work together as a system. The exile holds grief. The protector holds the exile back. "Moving on" often fails because neither part has been heard, only managed.

How to work with the part holding grief

  1. Find the part in your body. Sit quietly. Bring the grief to mind. Notice where in the body you sense something. Chest, throat, belly. That sensation is often where the part lives.
  2. Approach with curiosity, not urgency. Instead of trying to fix or release the grief, simply ask the part: "Can I get to know you a little?" Let it respond in any form: sensation, image, word, or feeling.
  3. Ask the protector for permission first. If you notice resistance, numbness, or a sense of being blocked, that is a protective part. Ask it: "What are you worried will happen if I get closer to this grief?" Listen without arguing with its answer.
  4. Earn the protector's trust. Thank the protector for working so hard. Let it know you are not trying to rip the grief away. You are asking to sit with the exile, not overwhelm it.
  5. Turn toward the exile. Once the protector steps back even slightly, turn toward the grief-holding part. Ask: "What do you want me to know? What have you been carrying?"
  6. Ask the key question. Slowly and sincerely ask the part: "If you let this grief move through, what are you afraid will happen?" This single question often reveals the actual burden: fear of forgetting the person, fear that releasing the grief means accepting the loss as acceptable, or fear of losing the last felt connection.
  7. Witness without fixing. Let the part show you what it has been holding. Your role here is presence, not resolution. Many parts just need to be seen for the first time.
  8. Invite, do not push, toward release. When the part feels fully witnessed, gently ask: "Is there anything you want to let go of? We can do that together, in whatever way feels right." This might involve imagery, a physical gesture, or simply a felt sense of the burden softening.

Signs the practice is working

  • The body sensation associated with the grief softens or shifts location
  • You feel a quality of presence with the loss rather than being overwhelmed by it
  • The protective behaviors (numbing, staying busy) feel less urgent
  • You can think about the person or loss without being flooded
  • A part of you feels heard for the first time, even if the grief is still there
  • The grief changes texture, from urgent and stuck to something more like ache or love

When to use IFS parts work for grief

This approach is most useful when grief feels frozen or looping, when you have tried "processing" the loss cognitively but still feel stuck, or when you notice yourself protecting against the grief through avoidance, numbness, or relentless forward motion. It is also helpful when the grief carries a particular belief, such as feeling guilty for surviving, feeling angry at the person who died, or feeling shame about how much you still miss someone. IFS can hold those complex parts without requiring them to be "fixed."

What the science says

IFS has moved steadily into clinical research. A 2021 randomized controlled trial by Hodgdon and colleagues, published in the Journal of Traumatic Stress, examined IFS therapy for adults with PTSD. The study found significant reductions in PTSD symptoms, depression, and self-compassion deficits compared to controls. The mechanism aligned with IFS theory: participants who increased Self-leadership, the quality of approaching one's own parts with curiosity and care, showed the largest gains. For grief specifically, the relevant finding is that Self-to-part attunement, the capacity to sit with a suffering part rather than flee from it, is itself therapeutic. The study's PubMed ID is 33252928.

Kenneth Doka's research on disenfranchised grief also supports the IFS framing. When grief is not acknowledged by the social environment, the system becomes more likely to develop protective strategies that lock the grief in place. IFS provides an internal recognition that the social environment fails to offer.

My experience with this

I came to IFS parts work after years of studying somatic therapies and realizing that the body-based work I was doing with clients kept running into a wall. The wall was usually a part that was very good at its job: keeping the grief locked down, keeping the person functional, keeping the loss at a manageable distance. I had learned to respect that wall. But IFS gave me a language for what was behind it.

The first time I used the "what are you afraid will happen if you let this through" question, the response was so specific it stopped me: "If I let the grief out, I'll have to accept that he's really gone. And I don't want to accept it." That part was not irrational. It was loyal. It was doing everything it could to hold something irreplaceable in place. Honoring that loyalty, rather than trying to dismantle it, was what finally allowed some movement. The grief did not go away. But the frozen quality did. That is the shift IFS reliably creates when it is given enough patience and Self-energy to work.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does it mean in IFS when you cannot let go of grief?
In Internal Family Systems, being unable to let go of grief usually means a part is actively holding it. That part has a reason, often it fears that releasing the grief means forgetting the person, accepting the loss as okay, or losing the last thread of connection. The holding is protective, not pathological.
What is the difference between an exile and a protector in IFS grief work?
The exile carries the raw pain of the loss. The protector, often a manager, keeps you from accessing that pain directly through numbing, staying busy, or intellectualizing. In grief work, you typically need to work with the protector first, earn its permission, and only then approach the exile holding the core loss.
Is IFS parts work safe to do alone for grief?
Self-led IFS is possible and described in Richard Schwartz's book No Bad Parts. The protocol in this article is a self-practice. If your grief involves severe trauma, complex loss, or feels destabilizing, working with a trained IFS therapist provides containment that self-practice cannot replicate.
What if the grief part refuses to talk or feels frozen?
A part that goes silent or feels frozen is usually a protector blocking access, not the exile itself. Turn toward the protector instead. Ask it what it is worried will happen if you get closer to the grief. This respects the system's pace and often creates more movement than pushing directly toward the pain.
Does IFS parts work for grief mean I have to relive the loss?
No. IFS does not require cathartic flooding. The approach is more like sitting beside a part and listening to it than re-experiencing the loss. You maintain a Self-to-part relationship, which means you are present with the grief without being consumed by it. The goal is witnessing, not re-traumatizing.
What does it mean for a grief part to be unburdened?
Unburdening means the part releases the extreme emotion, belief, or memory it has been carrying. After unburdening, the part is invited to take on a new quality in its place, often peace, lightness, or the capacity to remember the relationship without being overwhelmed by it. The grief does not disappear, it changes texture.
How long does IFS grief work take?
There is no standard timeline. Some people notice a shift in a single session. Others work with the same exile across many months. Complex, layered grief, especially involving trauma, tends to require longer. The pace is set by the protective system, not by any external schedule.
Can IFS parts work help with grief over a relationship ending?
Yes. IFS is not specific to death loss. Any significant ending, a relationship, a life phase, a career, or an identity, can leave parts holding grief. The same protocol applies: find the part in the body, ask permission to get closer, hear what it has been carrying, and explore what it needs to feel less alone.
