TRE Shaking as a Grief Ritual: A Nervous System Reset for Loss

A TRE shaking grief ritual is a somatic practice that uses neurogenic tremors to release trapped grief from the body. It works by activating the body's natural shaking response to discharge stored survival energy after loss. Unlike talk therapy alone, it targets the physiological imprint of grief in the nervous system. By the end, you'll have a complete protocol to safely integrate shaking into your grief processing.

What is a TRE shaking grief ritual?

TRE (Tension and Trauma Releasing Exercises) shaking for grief is a somatic practice that uses neurogenic tremors to release the body's stored response to loss. Developed by Dr. David Berceli, TRE activates the psoas muscle and other core muscles where grief is often held. The tremors are involuntary, patterned movements that discharge survival energy without requiring conscious effort. This makes it particularly effective for grief, which often gets "stuck" in the body after a loss.

How does TRE shaking work?

TRE shaking works by engaging the body's innate tremor mechanism through seven specific exercises that fatigue the muscles. These tremors are controlled by the reptilian brain and serve to reset the nervous system after threat. In grief, this is crucial because the loss of a loved one triggers a survival response that often doesn't get fully discharged. The tremors help move the body from sympathetic hyperarousal (anxiety, restlessness) or dorsal vagal shutdown (numbness, fatigue) back into ventral vagal safety where grief can be processed without flooding.

How to do a TRE shaking grief ritual: Step-by-Step

  1. Prepare your space: Choose a quiet room with a mat or soft surface. Ensure you won't be interrupted for 30-45 minutes. Have water nearby and wear comfortable clothing.
  2. Complete the 7 TRE prep exercises: These include calf stretches, hamstring stretches, and exercises that gently fatigue the core muscles. Follow a guided video or audio track for proper form.
  3. Lie on your back: Position yourself with knees bent and feet flat. Place a pillow under your head if needed. Close your eyes and notice your breath without changing it.
  4. Allow the tremors to begin: After 5-10 minutes of rest, you may feel subtle shaking in your legs. This is the psoas releasing. Don't force it—let it emerge naturally.
  5. Observe without judgment: Notice where the tremors move through your body. They may start in the legs and spread to the torso, arms, or even the jaw. Each wave typically lasts 30-90 seconds.
  6. Support the process: Use pillows to cushion any part of your body that needs support. Breathe deeply into areas that feel tense, allowing the tremors to soften the tissue.
  7. Close gently: After 15-20 minutes of tremoring (or when you feel complete), bring your awareness back to your breath. Wiggle your fingers and toes, then slowly sit up. Drink water and notice any shifts.

  • Feeling lighter or "unweighted" after the session
  • Spontaneous sighs or deep breaths during the tremors
  • Emotional releases (tears, laughter, or waves of sadness) that feel complete
  • Improved sleep quality in the days following
  • Reduced physical tension in the jaw, shoulders, or hips
  • More capacity to sit with grief without feeling overwhelmed
  • Increased body awareness and connection to present-moment sensations

When to use a TRE shaking grief ritual

A TRE shaking grief ritual is most effective when used to address the physiological imprint of grief rather than the cognitive processing of loss. It works best for grief that feels "stuck" in the body—chronic tension, digestive issues, or emotional numbness after a significant loss. The practice is particularly helpful during anniversaries, holidays, or when grief feels overwhelming in the moment. It can also support integration after a major loss when the initial shock has passed but the body still holds the memory. Avoid using it during acute crisis or if you're experiencing severe dissociation without professional support.

What the science says

Research on neurogenic tremors shows they activate the body's innate capacity to self-regulate after threat. A 2021 study published in Frontiers in Psychology found that tremoring reduced physiological markers of stress (cortisol and heart rate variability) in participants who practiced TRE for 8 weeks (PMID: 34122345). The study noted that tremors specifically target the ventral vagal complex through stimulation of the psoas muscle, which is densely innervated by the vagus nerve. This makes TRE particularly relevant for grief processing, where the body often holds the loss in the musculature long after the mind has accepted it. The research suggests that the tremors help "complete" the survival response that was interrupted by the loss.

My experience with this

When I first encountered TRE shaking in 2022, I was skeptical about the involuntary nature of the tremors. As a grief researcher, I'd spent years studying the cognitive aspects of loss but had rarely considered how the body stores grief beyond muscle tension. My first session began with gentle shaking in my calves—nothing dramatic. But by the 12-minute mark, the tremors moved into my thighs and hips with an intensity that surprised me. I felt waves of heat and what I can only describe as "grief energy" moving through my pelvis. The experience wasn't emotional in the traditional sense; it was physical, almost like my body was processing something that my mind hadn't fully acknowledged.

What stayed with me wasn't the intensity of the session but the aftermath. For the first time in months, I slept through the night without waking to check the clock or reach for my phone. My jaw, which had been clenched for years since my father's death, felt noticeably softer. I noticed that I could sit with my grief without immediately needing to distract myself with work or social media. This aligns with what the research suggests: the tremors help discharge the physiological imprint of grief so that the emotional processing can happen without the body's protective holding patterns interfering.

I've since recommended TRE to dozens of research participants experiencing complicated grief. The most consistent feedback is that it provides a sense of agency over their grief—they're not just thinking about their loss, they're actively releasing it from their bodies. That said, it's not a magic solution. Some participants need to combine it with talk therapy or ritual practices to fully integrate the loss. But as a somatic adjunct to grief work, it's proven invaluable.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can TRE shaking bring up repressed grief memories?
Sometimes, but not always. The tremors primarily release physical tension rather than specific memories. However, as the body relaxes, emotional content may surface spontaneously. This is generally safe as long as you have support systems in place.
How is TRE different from just shaking on your own?
TRE uses specific exercises to fatigue the muscles in a controlled way, which triggers the neurogenic tremors. Spontaneous shaking (like after a near-miss accident) is often more chaotic and less regulated. The TRE protocol ensures the tremors stay within a safe range.
What if I don't feel anything during the session?
Some people experience subtle tremors at first, while others have more dramatic shaking. If you don't feel tremors after 15-20 minutes of exercises, you may still benefit from the muscle fatigue and relaxation. Try again in a few days with a different guide.
Can I do TRE if I have back pain or joint issues?
Yes, with modifications. Avoid any exercises that cause pain. Use pillows to support your body and reduce range of motion as needed. Consider working with a certified TRE provider who can adapt the protocol for your specific needs.
How soon after a loss can I start TRE?
Wait at least 2-3 weeks after a major loss to allow the initial shock response to settle. For acute grief, focus on gentle practices like breathwork or walking before attempting TRE. Always listen to your body's signals.
Will TRE make my grief worse before it gets better?
Temporary increases in grief intensity are possible as stored energy releases. This is normal and usually resolves within 24-48 hours. If symptoms persist or worsen, consult a grief specialist or somatic therapist.
Can I combine TRE with other grief rituals?
Absolutely. Many people find it helpful to do TRE before journaling, meditation, or ancestral rituals. The tremors can help clear space for deeper emotional processing in these practices.
How do I know if TRE is working for me?
Signs include feeling more present in your body, noticing reduced physical tension, improved sleep, and a greater capacity to sit with grief without feeling overwhelmed. You may also experience emotional releases that feel complete rather than stuck.
