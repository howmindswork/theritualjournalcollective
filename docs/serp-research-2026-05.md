## Reddit Insights

From the polyvagal + grief Reddit data, real people are asking:

1. **"Is dorsal vagal shutdown the same as depression?"** — therapists on r/therapists actively debate whether polyvagal theory is scientifically valid, but practitioners still use it clinically. This creates an opportunity: content that acknowledges the debate while focusing on practical results.

2. **"How do I survive the early weeks of grief when I can't function?"** (r/GriefSupport) — the demand is for practical, in-the-moment tools ("how did you survive?"), not theory. People in acute grief are not reading long explainer articles. Short-protocol content (5 steps, under 3 minutes to read) matches this intent better than our current article formats.

3. **"My partner thinks I should be over grief by now — how do I explain this?"** — an enormous relationship-friction angle around grief timelines that nobody is targeting with ritual content. Content gap: "how to explain your grief nervous system response to your partner."

4. **"I'm a healthcare worker with grief + medical trauma — how do I process this?"** — specific occupational grief is underserved. Nurses, doctors, paramedics grieving patients is a very specific long-tail nobody owns.

5. **Polyvagal validity skepticism** — r/therapists debates whether dorsal vagal is scientifically supported. Our content should acknowledge Porges' theory as a practical framework (not proven neuroscience) and focus on the somatic experience, not the mechanism. This protects us from the "woo" criticism that could hurt trust signals.

Key insight: **Reddit grief communities are not using polyvagal or IFS language at all.** They say "I can't feel anything," "I'm stuck in bed," "I can't stop crying," "I feel numb." Our articles need to match this plain-language search intent in the first paragraph before introducing the technical terms.
